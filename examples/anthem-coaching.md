# Anthem Health Deal Coaching: Diagnosis & Recovery Protocol

**Opportunity:** Anthem Health  
**Deal Size:** $2.1M  
**Pipeline Duration:** 90 days  
**Current Status:** Stalled for 3 weeks  
**Stakeholders Engaged:** 1 VP of Data Analytics, 1 Director on her team  
**Recent Activity:** 2 follow-up emails sent, no response  

---

## Executive Summary

This deal is exhibiting classic **Wait State Collapse** — the champion has gone dark, momentum has evaporated, and the seller is trapped in a pure outreach loop with zero leverage. The diagnosis is not that the deal is lost. It is that the deal was never fully populated and the seller has no parallel workstreams to maintain velocity.

The seller's error is structural, not tactical. Two mistakes created this situation:

1. **Over-reliance on a single champion** with zero organizational depth
2. **Passive engagement strategy** (sending emails) instead of proactive orchestration

This is recoverable. But recovery requires fundamental repositioning, not more email.

---

## Diagnosis: Movement Assessment

### Where This Deal Actually Sits

The VP of Data Analytics saying "this is exactly what we need" appears to place the deal in **Value movement**. The demo was shown. The value was recognized. But this assessment is misleading.

**What actually happened:**
- One stakeholder, in one conversation, expressed enthusiasm
- No validation from other stakeholders
- No budget confirmation
- No procurement process initiated
- No competitive evaluation signals
- No internal alignment documentation

**True movement position: Familiarity + hopeful thinking**

The VP liked the solution. But liking something and committing an organization to a $2.1M decision are different things. The 3-week silence is the market telling you this deal has not entered Value movement. It has stalled in late Familiarity.

---

## Root Cause Analysis: The Wait State

### What Happened

90 days in pipeline, but only two real touches with decision-makers. The seller had one meeting with the VP and one with a director. Two meetings generated one positive signal. Then silence.

The seller's response: two follow-up emails.

This is a **Champion Silence wait state** — the classic pattern where one sponsor goes dark and the entire deal goes dormant because there is no one else to engage.

### Why This Happened

Three structural causes:

#### 1. Single-Threaded Dependency
The entire deal is routed through one person. If that person:
- Got busy with higher priorities
- Faces internal resistance she is not surfacing
- Is waiting for budget approval that has not come
- Believes she needs consensus before involving you further
- Is evaluating competitors without telling you

...then the deal stalls exactly as it has stalled.

The seller has zero visibility into which of these is true because there is no one else to ask.

#### 2. No Pre-Economic Validation
Before a VP commits a $2.1M budget, someone in finance or procurement needs to be engaged in the conversation. The seller skipped this step entirely.

The VP's enthusiasm means the *user* wants the solution. That is not the same as the *organization* being ready to buy.

#### 3. No Competitive Positioning
The seller has not confirmed whether Anthem is evaluating competitors. In a $2.1M deal that has been in pipeline 90 days, there is almost certainly competitive activity underway. The seller's silence about it suggests no competitive intelligence gathering occurred.

## Diagnosis: What Is Actually Happening at Anthem

### Three Scenarios (In Order of Likelihood)

#### Scenario 1: Consensus Bottleneck (Highest Probability)
The VP loved the solution. But she needs buy-in from:
- Her CFO or Finance VP (budget approval)
- Her CTO or Technical Lead (architecture validation)
- Her Chief Procurement Officer (contract, security, legal)
- Her CEO or COO (strategic alignment)

She is likely getting pushback from one or more of these stakeholders and has not looped you in to help overcome it. She may not even see the resistance as your problem to solve.

**Your leverage:** Identify the real blocker. You might not be the problem. Her inability to build consensus is.

#### Scenario 2: Competitive Evaluation
Anthem is evaluating you against 1-2 competitors. Your VP demo was impressive enough to not eliminate you, but not definitive enough to move past evaluation. Anthem is now running proof-of-value with a competitor or preparing a formal bake-off.

You are not being excluded because the deal is dead. You are being excluded because you are mid-evaluation and the VP is not surfacing the competitive activity because she does not want to pressure you or she does not yet know who will win.

**Your leverage:** Competitive acceleration. You need to surface whether evaluation is happening and, if so, move faster than the competitor.

#### Scenario 3: Budget Freeze or Strategic Shift
Something changed in Anthem's financial situation or strategic priorities in the last 3 weeks. The data analytics initiative is no longer a priority. Budget has been reallocated. The VP's political capital has diminished.

This is the hardest scenario because it is not about your solution. It is about Anthem's situation. But it is also the easiest to diagnose: one conversation with someone other than the VP will surface it.

**Your leverage:** Understanding what changed. Then either re-positioning the solution's value in the new context or disqualifying.

## Coaching: The Recovery Protocol

You have one objective: **Break the single-point-of-failure dependency and restore deal movement.**

This requires three parallel actions, starting immediately.

### Action 1: Diagnose the Real Blockage (24 hours)

**Goal:** Determine which scenario is true.

**Your move:**

Send a message to the VP. Not another email. **A phone call request with a specific frame.**

**Script:**

> Hi [VP Name],
> 
> I've reached out twice over the past three weeks and I suspect my emails may have gotten lost in your queue. More importantly, I want to make sure we haven't missed something — either on our end or on yours.
> 
> We've clearly resonated with you (your feedback after the demo was specific and enthusiastic), but enterprise decisions at your scale involve multiple stakeholders. I'm wondering if you've hit a particular gate in the approval process that I should know about — finance, technical architecture validation, procurement timeline, competitive evaluation timeline, or something else entirely.
> 
> I'm not trying to pressure you. I'm trying to be helpful. Three weeks of silence typically means one of three things: something changed in your world, someone internally wants proof before they commit, or you're evaluating alternatives. Each of those requires a different approach from us.
> 
> Can we grab 20 minutes this week so I can either unblock what's stuck or understand what I'm missing?

**Why this works:**
- You are naming the silence directly (shows you're paying attention)
- You are validating her initial enthusiasm (removes defensive posture)
- You are offering a hypothesis framework (makes it easy to answer — she picks which bucket it is)
- You are not asking for the deal. You are asking for information.

**If she does not respond:** Escalate within 48 hours (see Action 2).

---

### Action 2: Activate Secondary Stakeholders (Parallel, Immediate)

**Goal:** Stop waiting for one person to drive consensus. Get other stakeholders into the conversation directly.

**Your intelligence gap:** You do not know if the VP is experiencing resistance or just busy. Either way, you need to be in conversation with the other stakeholders.

**LinkedIn Research + Direct Engagement:**

Identify the likely approvers from these roles (LinkedIn search Anthem Health org):
- Chief Financial Officer or VP Finance
- Chief Technology Officer or VP Technology
- Chief Procurement Officer or VP Procurement

For each, find the most likely connection point:
- Mutual connections? (Ask the VP for an introduction)
- Company announcement or news mentioning them? (Reference it)
- Published statement on data analytics, cost reduction, or technology priorities? (Reference it)

**Sample outreach to Finance stakeholder:**

> Hi [CFO/Finance VP],
> 
> I noticed Anthem's recent earnings call emphasized the need for faster analytics and cost optimization in your operations. I've been working with [VP of Data Analytics] on a solution that directly addresses this (we demo'd it last month and her feedback was very positive — "this is exactly what we need").
> 
> I'm not sure if she's looped you in yet on the business case. Rather than wait for that to happen on the normal approval chain, I thought I'd reach out directly. A deal of this size and strategic importance shouldn't move purely through email.
> 
> One question: from a finance perspective, what would you need to see to move forward? Budget timing, ROI methodology, contract terms, risk mitigation structure — I want to make sure whatever we build aligns with how Anthem actually operates.

**Why this works:**
- You are positioning yourself as a problem-solver, not a pusher
- You are offering to solve *their* problem (finance, technical, procurement) not just selling to the VP
- You are moving from single-threaded to multi-threaded engagement
- You are creating parallel momentum

**Do not wait for the VP to introduce you.** If she has not looped in finance after 90 days, she is either blocked or not convinced it is her job to do so. Either way, you need to move independently.

---

### Action 3: Demonstrate Value Before They Ask (Parallel, 48-72 hours)

**Goal:** Move from "we think this would be good" to "here is what this actually looks like."

The VP gave you high praise. Demo was strong. But praise is not purchase. What she needs now is proof.

**Your move: Build a working POV or benchmark analysis using Anthem's public data.**

**What to create (choose based on deal stage and your resources):**

**Option A: Quick Benchmark Analysis (4-6 hours, if you have limited resources)**
- Anthem's Q3 data analytics operational metrics (from earnings call, investor presentations)
- Industry benchmark for comparable healthcare systems
- Specific gap analysis: "Based on your current analytics throughput and costs, here's how you compare to Meridian Health and CommonSpirit"
- Conservative ROI projection using Anthem's numbers

Share it as: "VP, after your 'this is exactly what we need' feedback, I wanted to ground that in real math before we move further. Here's what I've built from your public financial data — can you validate how close this is to your current state?"

**Option B: Working Prototype of Their Data (8-20 hours, if you can invest)**
- Pilot implementation using Anthem's de-identified claims data or similar
- Show actual performance improvement on their use case
- Build in their finance methodology, not yours

This is demonstration. This is what "show, don't tell" means.

**Why this works:**
- Removes the gap between "we love your solution" and "we are ready to proceed"
- Gives her something concrete to show her CFO: "Look what they actually built for us"
- De-risks the evaluation by reducing uncertainty
- Creates urgency: "We invested this time. What happens next?"

## Orchestration: Cross-Functional Mobilization

### Who You Need, When

**Immediately (within 48 hours):**
- **Finance or Deal Structure expert:** You may need to model creative payment terms or phased implementation that fits Anthem's budget cycle
- **Reference contact from a similar healthcare deal:** VP will eventually ask, "Have you done this at a healthcare system like ours?"

**Within 1 week (if secondary stakeholders engage):**
- **Technical architect:** If the CTO/Technology VP engages, they will have questions your demo cannot answer. You need someone who can speak architecture fluently.
- **Customer success from a healthcare implementation:** Once Anthem wants to evaluate implementation feasibility, success intelligence becomes critical

**Escalation (if still no movement after Action 1):**
- **Your VP/Director:** Anthem's VP may be more responsive to peer-level conversation than to yours. Consider having your leadership send a brief, specific note: "I know [our team] has been working with your data analytics team. Wanted to check in on any questions I can help address."

## Principles from FDS Doctrine Applied to Your Situation

### Innovation 1: Know More
**Your error:** You knew what the VP thought. You did not know what the organization thought.  
**Recovery:** Stop asking the VP for permission to engage others. Do your organizational research (LinkedIn, earnings calls, job postings, recent news) and proactively reach out to finance and technical stakeholders with specific, informed perspectives.

### Innovation 2: Hyper-Personalize
**Your error:** You personalized to one stakeholder. You built a context stack only for the VP.  
**Recovery:** Build stakeholder profiles for Finance VP, CTO, CPO (see stakeholder-profiles.md). Understand what each cares about. Speak to each in their language.

### Innovation 3: Demonstrate
**Your error:** You showed a demo. That was good. But you didn't demonstrate with Anthem's data.  
**Recovery:** Create a benchmark analysis or working POV using their actual numbers. Move from "here's what we can do" to "here's what we have done for you."

### The Three Principles Applied
- **Make It Easy to Say Yes:** Right now, saying yes requires the VP to build consensus alone. Remove that friction by engaging the other stakeholders directly.
- **Let Them See the Future:** Show them what their world looks like with this solution using their actual data, their metrics, their language.
- **Show, Don't Tell:** Stop emailing about capability. Deliver an actual working analysis or prototype.

## The Disqualification Path

**If this recovery protocol runs for 7 days and you get:**

- No response from VP to diagnosis call
- Finance saying "no budget available" with no path to change
- CTO saying "this doesn't fit our architecture" with no willingness to explore alternatives
- Competitive finding that you are late in an evaluation you don't control

**Then this deal is disqualified.** Not lost. Disqualified.

**How to handle disqualification with respect:**

> Hi [VP Name],
> 
> After our recent conversations, I think it's clear that [Anthem's timing / budget cycle / technical requirements / competitive positioning] means this isn't the right moment for us to work together.
> 
> That said, I'm still convinced we could deliver significant value for your organization. I'm going to stay in touch — not to push, but to support. If circumstances change, or if you hit a different problem we can solve, I want you to know you have a genuine advocate here.
> 
> In the meantime, if there's anything useful I can share with your team — research, benchmarks, introductions to peers facing similar challenges — just ask.

This keeps the relationship warm without false hope. Many deals disqualified today become pipeline tomorrow when circumstances shift.

## Summary

**The deal is not dead. The process is dead.**

You have three parallel moves:

1. **Get the truth** from the VP about what's blocking
2. **Go multi-threaded** by engaging Finance and Technical stakeholders directly
3. **Demonstrate value** using Anthem's actual data instead of generic capability slides

Execute these simultaneously over the next 7 days. You will either:
- Unlock the deal and move it forward at velocity
- Discover the real blocker and address it directly
- Realize the deal is disqualified and move on with credibility intact

The difference between a stalled deal and a lost deal is velocity. Restore velocity. Everything else follows.
