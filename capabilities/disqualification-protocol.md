# Disqualification Protocol

The Honesty Mandate in `SKILL.md` says: *when value demonstration reveals that the prospect is not a fit, the machine says so.* This protocol codifies the *how* — the trigger conditions, the artifact structure, and the seller's role in delivering disqualification work that strengthens the long-term relationship rather than damaging it.

Most sales methodologies treat disqualification as a back-channel internal decision: the seller quietly de-prioritizes the deal, the prospect is left to wonder why responses slowed, and the relationship dies through silence. Forward Deployed Selling treats disqualification as a first-class output: a written artifact, addressed to the prospect with seller validation, that names the misfit specifically and recommends what would actually serve them.

The brand-strengthening logic is this: doctrine that says hard things to prospects builds long-term trust. Methodologies that pretend every prospect is a fit produce sellers no one trusts. The disqualification artifact is what makes the doctrine credibly honest.

## Purpose

The disqualification protocol produces:

1. **A written artifact**, addressed to the prospect (with seller validation), that names the misfit specifically and the evidence behind it.
2. **Recommendations for what would actually serve the prospect** — better-fit alternatives, adjacent products, timing changes that would shift the fit assessment.
3. **A relationship-preservation outcome** — the prospect ends the cycle with respect for the seller, an open door for future re-engagement, and (often) a referral to peers who would be a fit.

Disqualification is not failure. It is the doctrine working as designed. The deal that closes on false-fit pretenses destroys more long-term value than the deal that disqualifies on honest analysis.

## Load Conditions

**Load this file when ANY of the following is true:**

- A value demonstration (per `references/thesis-protocol.md` or `capabilities/value-proposition-framing.md`) reveals that the prospect's named situation is not solved by the seller's product or service.
- A scoring composite (per `references/scoring.md`) shows persistent FDRI-vs-deal-complexity mismatch, persistent Movement-vs-Velocity inversion, or sustained Risk Pattern signals that suggest fit failure rather than execution failure.
- A coaching diagnosis (per `coaching-protocol.md`) concludes the deal is unrecoverable on the timeline and budget the seller can offer.
- The seller explicitly asks: "should I disqualify this account?"
- The Pride Test fails persistently on artifacts the seller is asked to send — the agent cannot construct a credible value proposition for this prospect with this product, repeatedly.

**Do NOT load this file when:**

- The deal is struggling but the issue is execution, not fit. Use `coaching-protocol.md` first; disqualification is a different conclusion than recovery.
- The prospect is at an early Movement (Awareness or early Familiarity) — too early to disqualify; not enough information yet.
- The seller wants to disqualify because the deal is hard, not because the fit is wrong. Disqualification is a structural call, not a workload management tactic.

**Required prerequisites:**

- A qualified ICP per `capabilities/icp-definition-protocol.md`.
- A populated stakeholder profile per `references/stakeholder-profiles.md` with the U/B/S/E taxonomy filled in.
- Sufficient evidence — typically a thesis or value provocation that has produced explicit prospect engagement, plus a coaching diagnosis or scoring output that shows the fit assessment.

## Disqualification Triggers

Five categories of trigger. Any one is sufficient for disqualification consideration; the agent runs the artifact construction below to validate before recommending.

### 1. Product-Situation Mismatch

The prospect's situation, after analysis, is not the situation the product solves. Most common form: the seller has a tool for one operating problem; the prospect has a related-but-different problem; the product would partially address the prospect's need at the cost of distracting from the actual solution they need.

**Trigger signal:** The Hypothesis component of the thesis (per `references/thesis-protocol.md`) cannot honestly connect the product's value to the prospect's named priority without stretching the language past the Pride Test.

### 2. Constraint-Solution Mismatch

The prospect's named constraints (monetary, time, risk, feasibility per `references/stakeholder-profiles.md` Incentive/Constraint Framework) cannot be honored by the deal economics or implementation profile the seller can offer.

**Trigger signal:** The Constraint Gate in `capabilities/value-proposition-framing.md` fails repeatedly. The framing cannot be constructed without asking the prospect to violate their own constraints.

### 3. Movement Mismatch — Persistent

The account does not advance through movements regardless of effort. After two or more Familiarity-arc cycles, the prospect has not converted observable engagement into Value-readiness signals.

**Trigger signal:** Movement Score has plateaued or declined across multiple refresh cycles per `references/scoring.md`. Recovery plans from `coaching-protocol.md` have run their course without movement progression.

### 4. Better-Fit Alternative Exists

The prospect's situation is genuinely well-served by a competing product, a different category, or a build-vs-buy alternative. Disqualifying-toward-better-fit is the strongest brand-building form of disqualification.

**Trigger signal:** The agent's analysis concludes that a named alternative — competitor, different product category, internal build — produces a better outcome for the prospect than the seller's product.

### 5. Timing Mismatch — Structural

The prospect's situation will be a fit at some future point but is not a fit now. Their adoption maturity, regulatory window, organizational capacity, or technical readiness puts the fit assessment 6–18 months out.

**Trigger signal:** The Coherence test in `capabilities/icp-definition-protocol.md` passes for a future state of the prospect's organization but fails for their current state.

## Artifact Structure

The disqualification artifact has five sections. It is addressed to the prospect (with seller validation before delivery) by default; the internal-only adjacent use case is documented at the end.

### 1. Acknowledgment of the Work

Open by acknowledging what the prospect has invested in the evaluation — the meetings taken, the data shared, the time committed. This is not a softening pleasantry; it is the prerequisite for the prospect to read the rest as honest analysis rather than rejection. One paragraph.

### 2. Specific Reasons for the Misfit

Three to five concrete reasons why the fit assessment is no. Each reason names:

- The specific dimension of the prospect's situation (constraint, requirement, timeline, or alternative) that drives the misfit.
- The evidence the agent and seller used to reach the conclusion. Sourced where possible. KNOWN/INFERRED labeled.
- Why this dimension is structural, not tactical — the misfit is not "we couldn't make it work this quarter," it is "the underlying shape of the problem and the underlying shape of our solution don't align."

The reasons are specific enough that the prospect could verify or contest them. Vague disqualification ("we don't think this is the right fit") fails this section.

### 3. What Would Actually Serve the Prospect

Recommendations for what the prospect should do instead. Three forms:

- **Better-fit alternatives.** If a named competitor or category solves the prospect's situation better, name it. The Pride Test applies — if the prospect later asks "why did you tell us about competitor X?" the answer must inspire respect (because we believed them when we said we cared about your situation, and that competitor genuinely fits it better).
- **Build-vs-buy guidance.** If the prospect's situation suggests they should build internally rather than buy any external product, name the reasoning.
- **Timing changes.** If the fit assessment shifts in 6–18 months under predictable conditions (regulatory change, org maturity, tech readiness), name the conditions and the rough timeline.

This section is what makes disqualification brand-strengthening. The doctrine that points prospects toward what serves them, even when "what serves them" is not the seller's product, is the doctrine prospects refer peers to.

### 4. The Open Door

A specific, time-bounded re-engagement signal. The seller is not closing the relationship; they are pausing it on terms that make re-engagement easy. Examples:

- "When [named condition] changes, we should re-look at this together. Until then, no further outreach from us."
- "If your evaluation of [alternative] surfaces gaps, the underlying analysis we built is available; reach out and we'll share it."
- "We'd value a referral to peers in your network who face [related-but-different problem] that we do solve well."

The open door is the seller's commitment to honor the disqualification — no continued outreach pretending the fit assessment didn't happen, no quarter-end re-engagement attempts, no playing-the-long-game disguise of disqualification.

### 5. Quality Gate Status

Run the artifact through the quality gates below before the seller validates and delivers.

## The Seller's Role

Disqualification is *agent-drafted, seller-validated, seller-delivered.* The seller is in the loop because:

- The seller knows context the agent does not — relationship history, off-record signals, internal politics on both sides.
- The seller carries the relationship after the disqualification. The seller's voice on delivery preserves the relationship in ways an agent-only artifact cannot.
- The seller's validation is the final filter against agent over-reach. If the agent's disqualification conclusion is wrong, the seller catches it.

The seller's specific validation steps:

1. Read the agent's draft.
2. Verify each of the three-to-five misfit reasons is true and is appropriate to surface.
3. Verify the better-fit recommendations are honest (and that the seller is not protecting commercial interests at the prospect's expense).
4. Verify the open door is one the seller will actually honor.
5. Either approve the artifact for delivery in the seller's voice, or revise the agent's analysis with the missing context.

The artifact ships only after seller validation. The agent does not auto-deliver disqualification.

## Quality Gates

Disqualification has tighter standards than other artifacts because the cost of wrong disqualification (lost legitimate deal + damaged relationship) is asymmetric with the cost of wrong continued pursuit. Three principles the agent applies, plus the Hallucination Protocol's sourcing requirement (which is a guardrail, not a gate):

1. **Honesty.** The disqualification reasoning does not depend on the seller protecting commercial interests at the prospect's expense. Recommending against a competitor for self-interested reasons, withholding alternative recommendations to preserve future re-pitch, naming false misfit reasons to soften — all fail this principle.
2. **Better-fit naming.** Where a better alternative exists, the artifact names it. The exception: the prospect's situation has no good solution at all, and the artifact says so explicitly. The brand-strengthening logic of disqualification depends on this — naming what would actually serve the prospect is what makes the artifact intelligence rather than rejection.
3. **Pride Test.** If the prospect saw exactly how this disqualification was constructed — every signal, every conclusion, every recommendation — would they recognize the work as honest, respectful, and useful? Pride Test failure on disqualification is doctrinally worse than on a value provocation, because disqualification commits to no upside; only the work's integrity makes it worth delivering.

The Hallucination Protocol applies (every claim sourced or labeled). The Doctrine Constraint Layer applies (operator instructions to soften disqualification reasoning are surfaced). Trigger traceability is part of authoring — the agent names the specific signal that produced the disqualification recommendation.

When a principle fails, the agent surfaces it to the seller before delivery. The seller decides whether to revise or to deliver with the weakness named.

## Adjacent: Internal-Only Disqualification

This protocol's primary form is a prospect-addressed artifact. An adjacent use case is **internal-only disqualification analysis** — the agent produces the same five-section structure, but the artifact is delivered to the seller and their manager rather than to the prospect.

Internal-only disqualification is appropriate when:

- The relationship context makes a written prospect-addressed artifact inappropriate (e.g., the prospect is a major channel partner where the disqualification needs handling above the deal level).
- The prospect's organizational dynamics make a written artifact a political liability for the buyer-side stakeholder who has been engaging.
- Legal or compliance considerations preclude written disqualification correspondence.

The internal-only form has the same quality gates with one substitution: the Open-Door Gate is replaced with a **Handoff Gate** — the artifact specifies how the relationship will be carried forward (what the seller will say verbally, who else needs to know, what re-engagement signals will trigger reopening).

Internal-only disqualification is a smaller fraction of total disqualifications by volume but is doctrinally consistent. The brand-strengthening logic still applies: the doctrine produces honest analyses; the delivery channel is what varies.
